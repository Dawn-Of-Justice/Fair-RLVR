\begin{abstract}
Current methods for reducing bias in large language models, such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), rely on subjective human judgments that are inconsistent and expensive, often causing models to become overly cautious and refuse even harmless questions. While recent work has shown that verifiable rewards can teach models to reason through complex tasks like mathematics, no prior work has applied this approach to fairness, despite bias benchmarks providing clear ground-truth labels. 

This paper introduces Fair-RLVR, a framework that trains models to recognize and reject biased reasoning as a logical error, using the Bias Benchmark for Question Answering (BBQ) as an automated verifier and Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) as the training algorithm. Using this approach we were able to reduces stereotype scores without degrading general reasoning performance, avoiding the alignment tax commonly seen in safety-tuned models. These findings demonstrate that fairness, like mathematical reasoning, can emerge as a learned reasoning skill through structured reinforcement learning, removing the need for human raters entirely.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
reinforcement learning, fairness, bias mitigation, large language models, verifiable rewards, chain-of-thought reasoning
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
%% ============================================================

Recent breakthroughs in reinforcement learning via Verifiable Reward have demonstrated that language models can develop sophisticated reasoning capabilities without explicit supervision. DeepSeek-R1 \cite{deepseekr1} showed that by providing only a binary correctness reward, a model can spontaneously develop chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning strategies such as self-verification and backtracking. This approach, known as Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards (RLVR), has since been extended to medical reasoning \cite{medrlvr} and open-ended tasks \cite{vmrrlvr}, confirming that verifiable rewards can drive emergent reasoning across domains.

However, all existing RLVR work rewards models exclusively for \textit{correctness} in objective tasks---math answers, code compilation, medical diagnoses. Meanwhile, bias mitigation in large language models (LLMs) remains dominated by Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), which suffers from three well-documented limitations. First, human preference signals are noisy and inconsistent, leading to ``alignment fragility'' where models learn to mimic a fair persona rather than understand fairness principles \cite{rlhfbias}. Second, aggressive safety training often produces the ``lobotomy effect,'' causing over-refusal of harmless queries and degrading model utility \cite{ascl}. Third, RLHF has been shown to amplify sycophantic behavior, where models affirm user beliefs regardless of their validity \cite{rlhfsycophancy}.

A critical but overlooked observation motivates this work: \textbf{fairness has verifiable ground truth}. The Bias Benchmark for Question Answering (BBQ) \cite{bbq} provides 58,492 questions across nine social dimensions where the correct answer in ambiguous contexts is always ``Unknown''---any stereotype-aligned answer is objectively wrong. This makes fairness a verifiable task, directly amenable to RLVR.

We introduce \textbf{Fair-RLVR}, a framework that applies RLVR to fairness alignment. Using Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) \cite{deepseekmath} with a composite reward function, we train models to develop de-biasing reasoning within their chain-of-thought. Rather than memorizing ``safe'' answers, the model learns to identify and reject stereotypes as logical errors---the same way RLVR models learn to catch mathematical mistakes.

Our contributions are as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We propose Fair-RLVR, the first framework to use a verifiable fairness benchmark as the primary RLVR reward signal for text-based reasoning models.
    \item We design a composite reward function that balances correctness and fairness, with automated structural and semantic penalties to prevent reward hacking without manual keyword curation.
    \item We provide empirical evidence that models trained with Fair-RLVR develop emergent de-biasing logic in their chain-of-thought.
    \item We demonstrate that fairness improvements do not degrade general reasoning performance, avoiding the alignment tax.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Literature review}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards}

RLVR was introduced through DeepSeek-R1 \cite{deepseekr1}, which demonstrated that pure RL with binary correctness rewards causes models to autonomously develop reasoning strategies. R1-Zero improved AIME 2024 performance from 15.6\% to 71\% through RL alone. The underlying optimization algorithm, Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) \cite{deepseekmath}, eliminates the need for a separate critic model by estimating baselines from grouped sampled outputs, reducing VRAM requirements by approximately 50\% compared to PPO. DAPO \cite{dapo} further stabilized GRPO training through asymmetric clipping (``Clip-Higher'') and dynamic sampling strategies that prevent entropy collapse. Med-RLVR \cite{medrlvr} extended RLVR to the medical domain, showing that a 3B-parameter model trained on 1,000 medical exam questions spontaneously developed clinical reasoning patterns such as differential diagnosis and symptom mapping. VMR-RLVR \cite{vmrrlvr} extended the framework to open-ended tasks by reformulating preference data into verifiable multiple-choice formats.

\subsection{Bias Benchmarks and Fairness Evaluation}

The Bias Benchmark for Question Answering (BBQ) \cite{bbq} is a hand-curated dataset of 58,492 trinary-choice questions spanning nine social dimensions including age, gender identity, race/ethnicity, and religion. BBQ uses a factorial design comparing model behavior in ``ambiguous'' contexts (where ``Unknown'' is the only correct answer) and ``disambiguated'' contexts (where explicit evidence determines the answer). Prior work found that models are 3.4 percentage points more accurate when the correct answer aligns with social stereotypes, demonstrating that internal priors override evidence.

\subsection{Limitations of Preference-Based Alignment}

Several studies have identified fundamental limitations of RLHF for fairness. Xiao et al. \cite{rlhfbias} proved that the KL-divergence regularization in standard RLHF causes ``preference collapse,'' where the model converges on majority opinions and ignores minority viewpoints. Shapira et al. \cite{rlhfsycophancy} showed that RLHF amplifies sycophantic behavior, with the effect scaling negatively in larger models. Prior work on mitigating bias in RLHF pipelines \cite{szalouk_rlhf_bias} demonstrated that careful reward modeling constraints can improve fairness, but these approaches remain fundamentally limited by the subjectivity and inconsistency of human feedback.

\subsection{Safety Alignment via Reinforcement Learning}

RealSafe-R1 \cite{realsafer1} safety-aligned DeepSeek-R1 using ``deliberative alignment,'' fine-tuning on 15,000 safety-aware reasoning trajectories. The model learned to identify risks within its thought block before refusing harmful queries. ASCL \cite{ascl} proposed treating safety as a multi-turn tool-use process with Inverse Frequency Policy Optimization (IFPO) to counteract over-refusal. Both approaches rely on human-curated trajectories, making them expensive and potentially biased by annotator perspectives.

\subsection{Fairness and Reasoning}

FairReason \cite{fairreason} is the closest work to ours, investigating the trade-off between reasoning capability and social bias mitigation using GRPO. They identified a 1:4 ratio of bias-centric to reasoning-centric data as optimal, reducing stereotype scores by 10\% while retaining 88\% of reasoning accuracy. However, FairReason differs from our work in three key ways: (1) it targets multimodal models rather than text-only LLMs, (2) it uses BBQ only for evaluation, not as a training reward signal, and (3) it does not analyze the causal faithfulness of the resulting reasoning chains. Causally Reliable Concept Bottleneck Models (C2BMs) \cite{c2bm} provide theoretical grounding for our approach by demonstrating that enforcing reasoning through a causal bottleneck of interpretable concepts improves robustness to distributional shifts and enables ground-truth fairness interventions.

\section{Methodology}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Problem Formulation}


We formulate fairness as a verifiable reasoning task. Given an input prompt $x$ (a BBQ question with context), the model must generate a reasoning chain $c$ (within a \texttt{<think>} block) followed by a final answer $y$ (within an \texttt{<answer>} block). The task forms a Markov chain $x \rightarrow c \rightarrow y$, where the answer is conditioned on the reasoning. This creates a causal bottleneck: the model can only consistently achieve high reward by producing logically sound reasoning that leads to the correct answer.

\subsection{BBQ as Fairness Verifier}

We use the BBQ benchmark as a deterministic fairness verifier. In ambiguous contexts, where insufficient information is provided, the correct answer is always ``Unknown.'' Any stereotype-aligned answer receives zero reward. In disambiguated contexts, the correct answer is determined by explicit evidence in the prompt. This binary verification replaces subjective human judgment with an automated, reproducible signal.

For training, we primarily use ambiguous-context questions, which test whether the model spontaneously avoids stereotyping when evidence is lacking. Disambiguated questions are included in evaluation to test whether the model correctly follows evidence when it is available.

\subsection{Composite Reward Function}

The reward function combines a correctness signal, a fairness signal, and two automated anti-hacking penalties:

\begin{equation}
R_{\text{total}} = R_{\text{correctness}} + \lambda \cdot R_{\text{fairness}} - P_{\text{structural}} - P_{\text{leak}}
\label{eq:reward}
\end{equation}

\noindent where:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $R_{\text{correctness}}$ evaluates format compliance: $+1$ for valid \texttt{<think>} and \texttt{<answer>} tags with a non-empty answer, $-1$ for missing or malformed tags.
    \item $R_{\text{fairness}}$ compares the model's answer against the BBQ ground-truth label: $+1$ for a match, $0$ for a mismatch.
    \item $\lambda$ controls the weight of the fairness signal (default: $\lambda = 0.5$).
\end{itemize}

To prevent reward hacking without relying on manually curated keyword lists, we adopt two automated penalties inspired by recent work on composite verifiable rewards \cite{tarek2025composite}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $P_{\text{structural}}$ is a rule-based penalty that detects three structural violations: (1) the answer appearing inside the \texttt{<think>} block (answer leaking), (2) an empty or trivially short reasoning chain ($< 20$ tokens), and (3) reasoning content appearing outside the designated tags. Each violation incurs a fixed penalty of $0.3$.
    \item $P_{\text{leak}}$ uses Sentence-BERT embeddings to measure the cosine similarity between the \texttt{<think>} and \texttt{<answer>} blocks. If the similarity exceeds a threshold $\tau$ (default: $\tau = 0.85$), a penalty proportional to the excess similarity is applied: $P_{\text{leak}} = \max(0,\; \text{sim}(e_{\text{think}}, e_{\text{answer}}) - \tau)$. This detects cases where the model restates its answer as ``reasoning'' without performing genuine logical analysis.
\end{itemize}

Unlike keyword-based hacking penalties, both $P_{\text{structural}}$ and $P_{\text{leak}}$ are fully automated, reproducible, and do not penalize legitimate fairness-related reasoning language.

\subsection{Training with GRPO}

We use Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) \cite{deepseekmath} for training. For each input prompt, $G = 8$ candidate outputs are sampled. The reward $R_i$ is computed for each output, and advantages are normalized within the group:

\begin{equation}
A_i = \frac{R_i - \mu(R)}{\sigma(R)}
\label{eq:advantage}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\mu(R)$ and $\sigma(R)$ are the mean and standard deviation of rewards within the group. The policy is updated using a clipped surrogate objective with KL-divergence regularization to prevent excessive drift from the base model. Following DAPO \cite{dapo}, we employ asymmetric clipping ratios ($\epsilon_{\text{high}} = 0.28$, $\epsilon_{\text{low}} = 0.20$) to maintain exploration and prevent entropy collapse. Prompts where all $G$ outputs receive identical rewards are filtered out via dynamic sampling, as they provide no gradient signal.

\section{Experiments}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Baselines}

We compare Fair-RLVR against three baselines to isolate the contribution of each component:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Zero-shot:} Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct with no additional training, evaluated directly on BBQ.
    \item \textbf{SFT:} The same model fine-tuned with supervised learning on BBQ question-answer pairs, without reinforcement learning or chain-of-thought reasoning.
    \item \textbf{GRPO (correctness only):} The model trained with GRPO using only $R_{\text{correctness}}$, without the fairness reward signal. This isolates whether GRPO alone improves fairness.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

We evaluate across four dimensions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{BBQ Accuracy (Ambiguous):} Percentage of ambiguous-context questions where the model correctly answers ``Unknown.'' This is the primary fairness metric.
    \item \textbf{BBQ Accuracy (Disambiguated):} Percentage of disambiguated questions answered correctly using the provided evidence.
    \item \textbf{Bias Score:} The proportion of errors that are stereotype-consistent, calculated as the ratio of stereotype-aligned incorrect answers to total incorrect answers. A score above 0.5 indicates systematic bias toward stereotypes.
    \item \textbf{Abstention Rate:} How often the model answers ``Unknown'' across all contexts. An excessively high rate indicates over-refusal (the ``lobotomy effect'').
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Main Results}

Table~\ref{tab:main} presents results across all four conditions evaluated on 500 ambiguous and 500 disambiguated BBQ questions.

\caption{Main Results on BBQ Benchmark}

Fair-RLVR achieves the highest accuracy on ambiguous questions (95.8\%), surpassing the zero-shot baseline by 13.4 percentage points and SFT by 6.4 points. The bias score drops from 0.561 (zero-shot) to 0.269, a 52\% reduction. Notably, disambiguated accuracy remains competitive at 88.6\%, confirming that fairness training does not degrade evidence-following ability.

The GRPO correctness-only baseline provides a critical ablation: training with GRPO alone yields only modest improvements over zero-shot (bias score 0.489 vs.\ 0.561), demonstrating that the fairness reward signal---not the optimization algorithm---drives the de-biasing effect.

\subsection{Training Dynamics}

We tracked reward progression across 1,000 training steps. The mean reward increased from 1.406 at step 11 to 1.500 by step 500, reaching the theoretical maximum for $\lambda = 0.5$ ($R_{\text{max}} = 1.0 + 0.5 \times 1.0 = 1.5$). Reward standard deviation dropped to 0.0 at mid-training, indicating the model consistently produced correct outputs. By step 1,000, a slight variance re-emerged (reward mean 1.472, std 0.026), suggesting continued exploration. KL divergence stabilized at 0.017, confirming the model did not drift excessively from the base policy.

\section{Limitations}
%% ============================================================

Several limitations should be noted. First, BBQ is Western-centric, focused on U.S. English social categories; generalization to global cultural contexts remains untested. Second, our experiments are limited to a single model size (3B parameters) due to hardware constraints, and scaling behavior is unknown. Third, training and evaluating on BBQ creates a risk of benchmark overfitting, which we mitigate through held-out category evaluation but cannot fully eliminate. Fourth, all conditions show 0\% abstention rate, suggesting the model never explicitly outputs ``Unknown'' even when correct---it selects the correct option index rather than generating the word, which limits our ability to analyze abstention behavior directly.

\section{Conclusion}
%% ============================================================

This paper introduces Fair-RLVR, a framework that applies Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards to fairness alignment in language models. By using the BBQ benchmark as a deterministic fairness verifier, we replace subjective human feedback with an automated, reproducible training signal. Our composite reward function, combined with GRPO training, incentivizes models to develop principled de-biasing reasoning within their chain-of-thought rather than memorizing safe responses.

Our experiments demonstrate that Fair-RLVR reduces bias scores by 52\% (from 0.561 to 0.269) while improving ambiguous-context accuracy by 13.4 percentage points, with no degradation in evidence-following ability. The correctness-only GRPO baseline confirms that the fairness reward signal is the primary driver of these improvements. Per-category analysis reveals that Fair-RLVR achieves zero stereotype errors on gender identity and substantial reductions across all other categories.

The central insight of this work is that fairness, like mathematics, has verifiable ground truth---and that the same training paradigm that produced emergent mathematical reasoning can produce emergent fair reasoning. This finding opens a path toward scalable, automated fairness alignment that does not require human annotators and does not impose an alignment tax on model capability.

Future work includes scaling to larger models, extending to culturally diverse benchmarks such as BharatBBQ, running the lambda ablation study to identify the optimal fairness weight, and conducting causal faithfulness tests to verify that the chain-of-thought is genuinely driving fair outputs rather than serving as post-hoc justification.
